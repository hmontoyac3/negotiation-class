SESSION 1 — THE OFFER

08:30 – 10:00 · 90 minutes · Distributive negotiation. Students are half asleep. Throw them straight in — minimum preamble, maximum doing.

08:30
Blackboard · Attendance
Open session + take attendance

Start Blackboard attendance first. While they're signing in, project the intro screen (the big €361K stat). Say nothing yet — let it sit.

08:35
Setup · 5 min
Explain the rules — max 3 minutes

Go to the Setup screen. Read the 3 rules out loud. Show the QR codes. "Left side of the room: scan the left code. Right side: scan the right code. Negotiate with the person next to you."

💬 Script: "You have a job offer. You're either the candidate or the HR manager. Your card tells you everything. You have 7 minutes to read it and use AI to prepare. Then I'm starting the negotiation timer and you talk. That's it."
08:40
AI Prep · 7 min
Students prep with AI — you watch

Start the AI Prep timer. Students read cards and use Claude/ChatGPT. Walk the room. This is data for your debrief: who looks at their phone immediately, who reads the card first, who doesn't use AI at all.

⚠ Don't help them with strategy. If anyone asks a question, say "Your card answers that" or "Ask AI."
08:50
Negotiation · 20 min
Live negotiation — all 60 pairs simultaneously

Start the Negotiation timer. The room gets loud. Walk constantly. You are looking for:

  • → Who makes the first offer, and how high/low is it?
  • → Anyone revealing their minimum? ("I really can't go below…")
  • → Anyone walking away? Anyone clearly caving under pressure?
  • → Any pair discovering the non-monetary trade? (remote work, start date)
These observations are your debrief gold. Make mental notes of 2-3 specific moments to reference by name ("I noticed one pair in the third row...")
09:15
Results · 10 min
Collect outcomes — live chart

Open the Results Board (keep session board in another window). Ask pairs to call out their final salary. Enter them one by one. The bar chart populates in real time — project it. "Who got above €2,800? Below €2,500? Anyone walk away?"

The chart itself is the lesson before the lesson. Let them see the spread. Say nothing yet.
09:25
Reveal · 10 min
Reveal the hidden information — click through concepts

Go to the Reveal screen on Session 1 board. Click each concept card one at a time — reveal BATNA, then ZOPA, then Anchoring, then Reservation Price. Ask the class to connect each one to what just happened to them.

⚠ The ZOPA reveal is the most powerful moment: "The candidate's minimum was €2,500. HR's ceiling was €2,900. There was a €400 window. Did you find it?" This is when they realize what information they were sitting on.
09:35
Debrief · 25 min
Structured debrief — 3 levels

Switch to the Debrief screen. Work through Level 1 (facts), Level 2 (analysis), Level 3 (transfer). Don't rush. The best debrief moment: ask "Who felt uncomfortable making a high first offer?" and wait for hands. Then: "What did that discomfort cost you?" — let them calculate it.

10:00
Break
15-minute break

Announce break. Tell them Session 2 starts exactly at 10:15 — same format, different scenario. Give no other preview.

The Numbers — Session 1 (Confidential)
▼

Candidate's cards: Target €2,800 · Minimum (reservation price) €2,500 · BATNA: consulting callback (uncertain, 6 weeks away — a weak BATNA). Wants: remote work (2 days), October start.

HR's cards: Opening offer €2,200 · Target €2,400 · Ceiling (budget cap) €2,900 · BATNA: second candidate at €2,500 but weaker. Can offer: remote work up to 3 days, flexible start, €500 training, 6-month review.

ZOPA: €2,500 to €2,900 — a €400 window.

The anchoring lesson: Candidates who open at €3,200+ will statistically land significantly higher than those who open at €2,700. The same information produces very different outcomes based purely on first number said.

The non-monetary trade: HR can offer remote work (costs nothing) + early review. Candidate values these. A deal at €2,600 + 2 remote days is better for the candidate than €2,700 with no flexibility.

SESSION 2 — THE RIGHTS

10:15 – 11:45 · 90 minutes · Integrative negotiation. Best energy of the day. Same format — but this time, most of them will miss the win-win. That's the lesson.

10:15
Blackboard · Attendance
Open session 2 — attendance

Start attendance. Project Session 2 intro: "Two companies. One athlete. One interview. Whoever pays more — wins." Let them read it. Say: "That's what you think."

10:18
Setup · 3 min
Same format, new roles

Show Setup screen. Same sides of the room as before, but new QR codes. "Left side: PodcastOne. Right side: VisualSport. Same 3 rules." No further explanation.

⚠ Do NOT say anything about the scenario being "different this time." Let them discover it.
10:21
AI Prep · 7 min
Students prep with AI — pay close attention

Start AI Prep timer. Walk the room more carefully this time. You're watching for: does anyone notice the "audio only" or "video only" detail? Do any pairs start prepping as if it's integrative? This is rare — most will be competitive from the start.

The key observation: AI will give both sides identical competitive advice. Note a few examples of what students got from AI — you'll use these in the debrief.
10:31
Negotiation · 20 min
Live negotiation — watch for the pivot moment

Start Negotiation timer. Walk the room. The critical thing to watch: the moment a pair stops competing and starts asking questions. That's the pivot to win-win. Note which pairs hit it and when (minute 5? minute 20? never?).

⚠ If a pair is clearly stuck in competitive mode at minute 15, you can prompt: "Have you asked each other what you're going to do with the interview?" — this is enough of a hint to see if they can find the win-win from there.
11:06
Outcomes · 5 min
Count outcomes — project the breakdown

Go to Results Board, Session 2 tab. Ask: "Who split the rights? Who had one side win? No deal?" Click the counters. The percentage bar chart updates instantly. Let them see the number.

11:11
Reveal · 10 min
The reveal — the most important moment of the day

Switch to Session 2 Reveal screen. Read the two role reveal cards aloud. Let the room absorb it. Then read the win-win box. Wait in silence for 5 seconds. Let the "oh" moment land.

The silence after the reveal is not awkward — it's the sound of learning. Don't fill it immediately.
11:21
Debrief · 24 min
Debrief — the AI meta-lesson is key here

Go to the Debrief screen. Work through the 4 levels, but make sure you hit the AI meta-lesson section: "Both cards had identical prompts. Both got identical advice. Why? What better question would have unlocked this?" — this is the most transferable insight from the whole day.

Key line to deliver: "AI gives you what you ask for. If you ask 'how do I win?' — it tells you how to win. If you ask 'what does the other side actually need?' — you get a completely different answer. The quality of your AI use depends on the quality of your thinking. The tool amplifies your frame. It doesn't give you a better one."
11:45
Break
15-minute break

Announce that Session 3 is short — attendance only + a 20-minute wrap, then they can go home early. This will get a very good reaction.

The Hidden Truth — Session 2 (Confidential)
▼

PodcastOne is audio-only. They need the audio recording and audio distribution rights. They have absolutely no use for video footage.

VisualSport is video-only. They need the video footage and video distribution rights. They have absolutely no use for audio rights.

The win-win: Split the rights. PodcastOne gets audio exclusivity. VisualSport gets video exclusivity. Both pay well under €30,000. Marco Costa earns from two separate deals.

Historical benchmark: In research settings, 30–40% of pairs discover this in similar scenarios. Most pairs never ask "what are you going to do with the interview?" — they assume identical needs because both say "we want exclusive rights."

The AI lesson: Both prompts say "How do I win?" — both get competitive advice. A better prompt: "I want exclusive interview rights. The other company also wants them. Before assuming we're competing — is there a way we both get what we need? What are some creative options?" That reframe changes everything.

SESSION 3 — THE WRAP

12:00 – 12:30 (target) · 90 minutes scheduled, but you're done in 30. Take attendance, do the wrap, let them go. They will love you for it.

12:00
Blackboard · Attendance
Open session 3 — attendance

Take Blackboard attendance. Announce: "This session is 30 minutes. We're going to wrap everything up, I'll leave you with the framework, and you're free."

12:05
Recap · 10 min
What you did today

Open Session 3 Wrap board, "What We Did" screen. Walk through the two sessions — distributive vs. integrative. Keep it brief. You're naming what they already lived, not teaching new content.

12:15
Framework · 10 min
The 3 Questions

Go to the "3 Questions" screen. Click through each question. Ask the class to connect each one to what happened in Sessions 1 and 2. Don't lecture — ask. "Where did this appear this morning?"

12:25
Done
Go to the Done screen — let them go

Click to "Done" screen. Read the three reminder lines. Say: "That's it. You negotiated today. You'll negotiate for the rest of your career. The only difference is now you know why things happen the way they do." Let them go.

WHAT TO WATCH FOR
Session 1 — What to observe during negotiation
▼
  • Who speaks first? In most pairs, the candidate opens. The one who anchors first and boldest usually lands higher.
  • Who reveals their minimum? "I really can't go below €2,500" — this happens in roughly 30-40% of pairs. Note it for the debrief.
  • Who caves at HR's first refusal? "The budget is fixed at €2,200" — some candidates fold immediately. Others push back. Contrast them in debrief.
  • Non-monetary trades: Rare, but beautiful when they happen. A pair that discovers "€2,600 + 2 remote days" instead of fighting to €2,800 has found an integrative solution inside a distributive scenario.
  • Emotional reactions: Watch for discomfort. Some students will literally be unable to name a high number. That discomfort is the lesson.
Session 2 — What to observe during negotiation
▼
  • The pivot moment: When does a pair stop bidding and start asking questions? The earlier, the more likely they find the win-win.
  • Who asks "what are you going to do with it?": This is the magic question. If anyone asks it in the first 5 minutes, highlight them in the debrief.
  • The bidding war: Most pairs escalate price rapidly. Some will hit their €60K ceiling, declare impasse, and still not discover the win-win.
  • AI copy-paste behavior: Some students will show you their AI response. Note the advice it gave — "anchor first," "be firm on price" — for the debrief contrast.
  • The "we can split it" discovery: When a pair finds it, watch their body language. There's usually a visible moment of surprise, then laughter. These are your debrief examples.
IF THINGS GO WRONG

Students finish too fast (under 15 min)

Tell early finishers to try again with swapped roles (candidate becomes HR). Or give them a reflection prompt: "Write 3 things you'd do differently if you could restart." This generates debrief material.

Almost nobody finds the win-win in Session 2

This is actually fine — and historically common. The lower the win-win rate, the more powerful the reveal. Don't be worried if it's 10%. Say in the debrief: "30-40% find it in research settings with trained negotiators. You got X%. What does that tell you about how we negotiate by default?"

Students don't use AI during prep time

Don't force it. Use it as a debrief question: "Who didn't use AI? How did that affect your preparation?" The contrast between those who prepped with AI and those who didn't is itself data.

The single most important thing for the debrief

Don't explain what happened — make them explain it. For every concept (BATNA, anchoring, positions vs. interests), ask "who can give me an example of this from what happened this morning?" and wait. The best debrief answers are always student answers, not professor answers.
