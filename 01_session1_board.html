WHAT JUST HAPPENED
BATNA
Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement — your real source of power
The candidate's BATNA: the consulting firm callback — uncertain, 6 weeks away. A weak BATNA.

The HR manager's BATNA: the second candidate at €2,500 — weaker, but real.

The lesson: Whoever had the stronger BATNA had more power. But most people never ask: "What happens if I walk away?" If you don't know your BATNA, you have no anchor for your decisions.

ZOPA
Zone of Possible Agreement — the overlap where a deal can happen
The ZOPA in this negotiation: €2,500 (candidate's minimum) to €2,900 (HR's ceiling) = a €400 window.

If the candidate didn't know their minimum, they might accept €2,200. If HR didn't know their ceiling, they might stop at €2,400 when the candidate was still in the room.

The lesson: A ZOPA exists — but only the people who prepared found it. No ZOPA = no deal. Know yours before you start.

Anchoring
The first number said in a negotiation disproportionately shapes the final outcome
Look at the class results: Candidates who opened at €3,200 landed significantly higher than those who opened at €2,700 — even though both had the same cards.

The first number creates a gravitational pull. Counteroffers cluster around it. The other side adjusts from it, not from their own number.

The lesson: Go first when you're prepared. Anchor boldly but justify it. "I was thinking €3,200, based on market rates for this role in Milan."
↓ Click to reveal
Reservation Price
Your absolute walk-away point — never cross it, never reveal it
The candidate's reservation price: €2,500. Below that, wait for the consulting firm.

HR's reservation price: €2,900. Above that, their budget is broken.

The most common mistake: Saying "I really can't go below €2,600" — which just moved your reservation price from €2,500 to €2,600 in the other person's mind. They'll push to exactly €2,600 and stop.

The lesson: Know it. Don't cross it. Never say it.

The Key Insight

Both sides had the same facts. The difference in outcomes came entirely from preparation (knowing your numbers) and anchoring (who moved first and by how much). AI helped you prepare — but you had to be the one to sit across from another human and execute under pressure.

THE DEBRIEF
Don't explain what happened — help them articulate it. Wait after each question. The best answers take 15 seconds to come. That silence is learning.
LEVEL 1 — What happened? (Facts from the room)
Who made the first offer? How did it feel to be the one to go first?
Who got a deal above €2,700? Below €2,500? Show of hands.
Did anyone walk away without a deal? What made you decide that?
Did anyone reveal a number they wish they hadn't?
LEVEL 2 — Why did it happen? (Analysis)
What did your AI advice say? Did it help? What did it get wrong?
Why does the first number have so much power? Where else in life does this happen?
What question — if you had asked it — would have changed the negotiation completely?
Who felt uncomfortable making a high first offer? Why? What did that discomfort cost you?
LEVEL 3 — So what? (The principle)
You graduate in June. This conversation is happening in September. What do you do differently?
Name one thing that would have been different if you had spent 20 more minutes preparing.
The other person had confidential information you didn't know. In real life — how do you find out what you don't know?
💡 Key concept to land before moving on: In a distributive negotiation, the deal happens inside the ZOPA — but only the person who prepared for it can find it. AI gave them a strategy. The live conversation required something else: composure, judgment, reading another human. That's the part that doesn't go away.
