WHAT THEY ACTUALLY NEEDED
PodcastOne

An audio-only network. They needed audio recording and distribution rights.

They had zero use for video footage. None. Their studio doesn't do video.

VisualSport

A video-only channel. They needed video footage and video distribution rights.

They had zero use for audio rights. None. Their team doesn't do podcasts.

The Win-Win That Was Available from Minute One

PodcastOne + VisualSport could have split the rights: audio to one, video to the other. Both get exactly what they need. Marco Costa earns from both deals. Neither side spends anywhere near €60,000.

Most pairs never found this — because they were competing on position ("we want the rights") instead of exploring interest ("what exactly do you need them for?").

The single question that unlocks it: "What are you going to do with the interview?"

THE DEBRIEF
This one is more important than Session 1. What they just experienced is the fundamental insight of integrative negotiation — and most of them missed it. That's fine. That's the lesson.
LEVEL 1 — What happened? (Facts from the room)
How many pairs split the rights? What made you think to ask about it?
For those who didn't find it — at what point did you realize the win-win existed? How did it feel?
What was the first question you asked your partner — and what did they say?
Did anyone ask "what are you going to do with the interview?" At what minute?
LEVEL 2 — Why did it happen? (Analysis)
Both cards said "exclusive rights." Why did that word make you assume the other side needed the same thing as you?
What's the difference between a position and an interest? Use this scenario to explain it.
Why did the AI not help you find the win-win? What would you have needed to ask it?
The pairs who found the win-win — what did they do differently in the first 5 minutes?
LEVEL 3 — So what? (The principle)
Name a real situation where two people are fighting over something — and it turns out their actual needs don't conflict at all.
In your career, you'll negotiate budgets, resources, timelines, roles. How do you find out what the other side actually needs — not what they're asking for?
Positions divide. Interests connect. Give me one example from today where you saw that happen.
THE AI META-LESSON
Both cards had identical AI prompts. Both got identical advice: "anchor first, compete hard." Why?
What would a BETTER AI prompt have looked like — one that actually led you to the win-win?
What does this tell you about how to use AI in any negotiation? What's the question you should always ask it?

The AI Lesson to Land

Both parties got the same AI advice because they asked the same question: "How do I win?"

AI reflects the frame you give it. If you ask it to help you compete, it helps you compete. If you ask "What might the other side actually need? What creative options exist?" — you get integrative thinking.

The quality of your AI use depends entirely on the quality of your questions. And the quality of your questions depends on how well you already understand negotiation. The tool amplifies the thinking. It doesn't replace it.

💡 To close both sessions: In Session 1, you learned that preparation wins in competitive negotiation. In Session 2, you learned that asking "why" wins in collaborative negotiation. The real skill is knowing which situation you're in — and AI can help you prepare for both, if you ask it the right questions.
